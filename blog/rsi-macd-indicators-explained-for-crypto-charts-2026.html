What Are RSI and MACD Indicators in Crypto Analysis?

RSI (Relative Strength Index) — The Momentum Gauge

The Relative Strength Index (RSI) is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and change of price movements on a scale of 0 to 100. Developed by J. Welles Wilder in 1978, it remains one of the most widely used technical indicators in cryptocurrency analysis.

How RSI works: - Values above 70 suggest an asset may be overbought - Values below 30 suggest an asset may be oversold - The standard period setting is 14 (meaning it looks back at 14 candles)

For crypto traders in Germany, RSI helps answer a critical question: “Is this price move running out of steam?” When Bitcoin surges 15% in a day and RSI hits 85, the indicator signals that buying pressure may be exhausted — not a guaranteed reversal, but a data point worth understanding.

MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence) — The Trend and Momentum Tool

MACD, created by Gerald Appel in the 1970s, combines trend-following and momentum analysis into one indicator. It consists of three components:

Component What It Shows How to Read It
MACD Line (12-period EMA minus 26-period EMA) Short-term momentum relative to long-term Rising line = increasing momentum
Signal Line (9-period EMA of MACD) Smoothed version of MACD Cross above = bullish signal; cross below = bearish signal
Histogram (MACD Line minus Signal Line) Strength of momentum Bars growing taller = momentum accelerating

Why These Two Indicators Work Together

RSI and MACD complement each other perfectly:

  • RSI excels at identifying extreme conditions (overbought/oversold) but gives false signals in strong trends
  • MACD excels at trend direction but lags behind price action
  • Together, they provide a more complete picture: RSI tells you how extreme the move is, while MACD tells you where the trend is heading

A concrete example: When Bitcoin’s RSI drops to 28 (oversold territory) and the MACD line crosses above the signal line, you have two independent indicators suggesting the same thing — potential upward momentum. That’s stronger evidence than either indicator alone.
